Mueller Polarization Imaging

Polarimetric imaging is a type of imaging using the polarization of light as a source of information.It is based on the reflection and transmission properties of the different objects studied, which modify an initial polarization state.

This type of imaging thus makes it possible to obtain information on the microscopic structure of objects.For polymers, this information is particularly interesting, because the same monomer can give different polymer structures.

It also makes it possible to separate multiple reflections in depth of an object from reflections on the surface.Polarimetric imaging can be used as a 'filter' in order to retain only the desired information.

Polarization:

A light wave is an electromagnetic wave consisting of an electric field (E) and a magnetic field (B) oscillating perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.

In natural light, such as that of the Sun, these oscillations occur in all directions perpendicular to the direction of propagation: we speak of 'unpolarized' light.Light is said to be 'polarized' when it only vibrates in certain specific directions.

Polarization can be linear (vibrations in a single direction), circular (oscillations describing a circle around the axis of propagation), or elliptical (oscillations following an ellipse).

Stokes formalism:

To fully characterize the polarization of light, we generally use the Stokes formalism, represented in the form of a vector:

$$ S = \begin{bmatrix} I \\ Q \\ U \\ V \end{bmatrix} ... $$

Mueller matrix:

The Mueller matrix M_object defines the transformation of a vector S_init into a vector S_output after interaction with an object or optical element.

$$ S_{sortie} = M_{objet}.S_{init} $$

Measuring principle:

The incident polarization is generated by a PSG (Polarization State Generator), the object is studied, and the output state is analyzed by a PSA (Polarization State Analyzer).

PSG and PSA:

The PSG makes it possible to obtain all the desired polarizations.The PSA, consisting of the same optical elements but in reverse order, analyzes the output state.
